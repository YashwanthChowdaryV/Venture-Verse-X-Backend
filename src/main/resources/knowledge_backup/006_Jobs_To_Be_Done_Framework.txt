SUBCATEGORY: Customer Discovery

STAGE: Ideation, Validation
PERSONAS: Founder, Investor, Product Strategist

CORE CONCEPT

Jobs To Be Done (JTBD), developed by Clayton Christensen and refined by Bob Moesta, is a causal model of customer behavior that explains purchase decisions not through customer attributes but through the functional, emotional, and social progress a customer seeks to make in a specific circumstance. The core concept is that customers do not buy products; they hire products to make progress in their lives. The unit of analysis is the job, not the customer. The failure mode is "Solution-Centric Development": founders who optimize their product against competitor features rather than against the job the customer is trying to accomplish. A product that perfectly executes an irrelevant job will fail; a mediocre product that nails a high-stakes job will thrive. For investors, JTBD is a lens for distinguishing between companies that understand the causal mechanism of demand (why customers pull a product into their lives) and those that simply observe correlation (who is buying and when). A founder who can describe the struggling moment when a customer fires the old solution and hires the new one has demand-side insight that cannot be copied by a well-funded competitor.

FRAMEWORK & MECHANICS

The JTBD framework operates through the Forces of Progress model, articulated by Bob Moesta. It describes the four forces that govern a customer's decision to switch: Push (the dissatisfaction with the current solution that pushes the customer toward change), Pull (the attractiveness of the new solution that pulls the customer toward adoption), Anxiety (the fears about the new solution that inhibit switching), and Inertia (the attachment to the current solution that maintains the status quo). A hire occurs when Push + Pull > Anxiety + Inertia. The mental model is the customer as a hiring manager, evaluating candidates against the job description. The job is not a task; it is the progress the customer seeks. A functional job might be "transport me from point A to point B safely and on time." The emotional job might be "make me feel valued and respected during my commute." The social job might be "signal my environmental consciousness to my peers."

The operational mechanism is the Job Story format, an alternative to user stories: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]." This forces specificity about the context—the struggling moment—that triggers the search for a new solution. The discovery process is the JTBD interview, a structured conversation that reconstructs the moment of purchase: what triggered the search, what alternatives were considered, what trade-offs were made, and what "hiring criteria" emerged. The decision process requires mapping the full competitive set, which includes non-consumption (doing nothing) and workarounds (manual processes, spreadsheets). A startup's true competitor is not the other product in the category; it is the solution the customer currently uses to make progress, even if that solution is "nothing."

KEY METRICS & BENCHMARKS

The primary JTBD metric is Job Importance Score: the percentage of target customers who describe the job as a top-3 priority in their work or life. Benchmark: a viable market requires a Job Importance Score above 40% in the target segment. A job rated as "nice to have" by over 60% of the target segment does not generate the pull force necessary to overcome switching inertia. The second metric is Current Solution Dissatisfaction: the percentage of target customers who rate their current solution as a 6 or below on a 10-point satisfaction scale. Benchmark: above 50% dissatisfaction is required for a pull-based go-to-market; below 30% requires heavy marketing spend to manufacture urgency. The third metric is Switching Trigger Frequency: how often the struggling moment occurs. A job with daily triggers (communication, scheduling) has higher venture potential than a job with annual triggers (tax filing, insurance renewal). The fourth metric is Willingness-to-Pay Multiplier: the ratio between what customers currently spend on their workaround or alternative and what they would spend on a dedicated solution. A multiplier above 3x indicates a job with significant unmet demand.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

The now-famous milkshake case study from Clayton Christensen exemplifies JTBD thinking. A fast-food chain sought to improve milkshake sales using standard customer profiling—demographics, preferences, attributes—and saw no improvement. Bob Moesta's team applied JTBD interviews and discovered that 40% of milkshakes were purchased before 8:00 AM by solo commuters. The job the milkshake was hired for: make a long, boring commute more interesting while keeping the commuter full until 10:00 AM. The competitive set was not other milkshakes; it was bananas, bagels, doughnuts, and boredom. Bananas failed because they were consumed too quickly. Bagels failed because they required cream cheese and attention. Doughnuts failed because they didn't provide lasting fullness. The milkshake won because its viscosity extended the consumption time, fitting perfectly into a 20-minute commute. The outcome: the chain thickened the morning milkshake and added fruit chunks to extend engagement, increasing morning sales by a reported 7x. The investor lesson: the chain's initial customer research asked "how can we make a better milkshake?"—the wrong question. JTBD asked "what job is the milkshake being hired for?"—the right question. Startups that optimize their product without understanding the job it is hired for are solving a different problem than their customers have.

COMMON MISTAKES

The first mistake is "Job as Task": reducing the job to a functional task description ("send an email") rather than the progress sought ("maintain a professional relationship without investing time I don't have"). Functional jobs are easy to copy; emotional and social jobs create defensibility. The second mistake is "Solution Tunneling": conducting customer interviews that ask about the product rather than the struggle. Founders ask "what features do you want?" instead of "when was the last time you faced this problem and what did you do?" The former generates feature requests; the latter generates demand signals. The third mistake is "Segmenting by Persona Rather Than Job": organizing customers by demographic or firmographic attributes rather than by the job they are trying to accomplish. A 25-year-old marketing manager and a 55-year-old CFO may both be hiring a tool for the job "prove the ROI of my decisions to my leadership." The fourth mistake is "Ignoring the Competitive Set of Non-Consumption": dismissing the customer who solves the problem with a manual workaround as "not a real customer" rather than recognizing them as the richest source of demand insight. A customer who built their own spreadsheet solution has demonstrated the highest possible Job Importance.

COUNTERINTUITIVE INSIGHT

Your best customers are not your most satisfied users; they are the users who most recently switched from an alternative solution. Satisfied users can describe what they like; recent switchers can describe why they hired the product, what they fired, and what they almost hired instead. The highest-signal customer research is conducted with customers who purchased within the last 60 days, not with long-tenured power users.

INVESTOR PERSPECTIVE

Positive signals include a founder who can describe the specific struggling moment of their best customer with more precision than they describe their product roadmap. A JTBD interview database with 20+ documented switching narratives is more valuable than a 50-page market research report. Red flags include market sizing that ignores non-consumption as a competitor, and a product strategy organized by competitor feature parity rather than job completion. Due diligence questions: "Describe the last 3 customers who evaluated your product but chose not to buy. What job were they trying to accomplish, and which competing solution did they hire instead?" "What is the DIY workaround your best customers used before finding you, and how much did that workaround cost them in time or money?" "If your product disappeared tomorrow, what would your customers do to make the same progress?" The last question reveals whether the job is real. If the answer is "they'd just go back to the old way," the pain of the old way was insufficient to drive a hire.

VENTUREVERSE EVALUATION SIGNALS

VentureVerse agents must evaluate JTBD depth as a signal of demand-side understanding. Positive Signals: Documented switching narratives from 15+ customers using the JTBD interview format; a competitive set that includes manual workarounds and non-consumption; a product roadmap organized around job completion rather than feature parity; evidence of the Forces of Progress model in the go-to-market strategy; a customer segment definition based on job context rather than demographic attributes. Warning Signals: Customer research limited to surveys and Net Promoter Score; a founder who cannot name the last customer who switched from a competitor and the specific trigger event; market sizing that assumes 100% conversion of a TAM without analyzing the Anxiety and Inertia forces; a product described as "better" rather than described as enabling a specific progress the customer cannot achieve otherwise. Scoring Signals include a JTBD Maturity Score based on interview depth, switching narrative volume, and competitive set sophistication. Risk Indicators: A product strategy driven entirely by feature requests rather than job analysis; customer retention driven by switching costs rather than job completion quality.

ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS

1. Conduct 10 JTBD interviews with customers who purchased in the last 60 days; document the specific struggling moment, the alternatives considered, and the trade-offs made.
2. Map your competitive set to include non-consumption, manual workarounds, and indirect substitutes; identify the job each competitor is hired for and the specific force (Push, Pull, Anxiety, Inertia) that most influences the switching decision.
3. Rewrite your product roadmap as a Job Completion Roadmap, where each initiative is tied to removing an obstacle to completing the job, not adding a feature.
4. Identify the emotional and social dimensions of the job your product is hired for; if you can only describe the functional job, your value proposition is undifferentiated.
5. Create a Switching Timeline for your last 10 customers: document the date of the struggling moment, the date of first consideration, and the date of purchase. Analyze the average duration to identify go-to-market timing opportunities.
6. Replace your "Target Market" slide with a "Job Specification" slide describing the exact circumstance, motivation, and expected outcome of the customer who hires your product.
