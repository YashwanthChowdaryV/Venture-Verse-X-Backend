TITLE: Convertible Notes
DOCUMENT_ID: 028
CATEGORY: Venture Capital & Fundraising
SUBCATEGORY: Financing Instruments
DIFFICULTY: Intermediate
STAGE: Pre-Seed / Seed
PERSONAS: Founder, Investor, Venture Analyst, Legal Counsel

CORE CONCEPT

A Convertible Note is a debt instrument that converts into equity at a future priced financing round, typically with a Valuation Cap, a Discount Rate, and an Interest Rate. It is the predecessor to the SAFE and remains the dominant Seed instrument in many non-US markets and in situations where investors prefer the structural protections of debt. The core concept is "bridge financing": the Convertible Note bridges the company from its current state to a priced equity round, providing capital now with conversion terms that are set relative to the future round. The failure mode is "Note Maturity Default": unlike a SAFE, a Convertible Note has a maturity date (typically 12-24 months) at which the note must be repaid or renegotiated if no qualified financing has occurred. A company that fails to raise a priced round before the note matures faces a cash repayment obligation it typically cannot meet, creating a distressed situation where the note holders have significant leverage. For investors, Convertible Notes provide stronger protections than SAFEs — the debt structure, interest accrual, and maturity date create a security interest that SAFEs lack — at the cost of greater legal complexity and the potential for an adversarial relationship with the company if the note approaches maturity without conversion.

FRAMEWORK & MECHANICS

The Convertible Note operates as a loan that converts to equity upon a Qualified Financing (a priced equity round above a specified threshold). The key terms are: Principal (the amount invested), Interest Rate (simple interest accruing on the principal, typically 2-8% annually, paid in equity at conversion, not in cash), Maturity Date (the date by which the note must be repaid if not converted, typically 12-24 months from issuance), Valuation Cap (the maximum valuation at which the note converts, same function as the SAFE cap), and Discount Rate (a percentage discount on the Qualified Financing price, typically 10-25%). At conversion, the note's principal plus accrued interest converts into the Qualified Financing shares at the lower of the Cap price and the Discount price.

The conversion mechanics: Conversion Amount = Principal + Accrued Interest. Conversion Price = min(Cap Price, Discount Price). Number of Shares = Conversion Amount / Conversion Price. If the Qualified Financing pre-money valuation is $40M, the note cap is $10M, the discount is 20%, and the principal plus interest is $1.1M: the Cap Price gives a conversion price of ($10M / Fully Diluted Shares) and the Discount Price gives ($40M * 0.8 / Fully Diluted Shares). The Cap Price is lower, so the note converts at the Cap Price. The maturity date creates a negotiation event if no qualified financing has occurred. The standard provisions are: the note can be extended by mutual agreement, the note can convert at the cap in a non-qualified financing, or the note becomes due and payable. The mental model is "Convertible Note = SAFE + Debt Structure + Maturity Date." The debt structure provides the investor with creditor rights in a bankruptcy or dissolution scenario, and the maturity date provides a forcing mechanism for a financing or a conversation about the company's status.

KEY METRICS & BENCHMARKS

The primary Convertible Note metric is the Effective Dilution at conversion, calculated identically to SAFE dilution: the percentage of the company the note holders receive upon conversion at the Cap or Discount. Benchmark: cumulative note dilution (if multiple notes) below 20% is clean; 20-30% is manageable. Warning threshold: above 30% cumulative note dilution. The second metric is the Interest Rate. Benchmark: 2-8% simple interest annually. Warning threshold: interest rates above 10% are uncommon in venture and signal a distressed financing. The third metric is the Maturity Date. Benchmark: 18-24 months from issuance provides sufficient runway to achieve a qualified financing. Warning threshold: a maturity date less than 12 months from issuance creates a high probability of maturity before a qualified financing is achieved. The fourth metric is the Qualified Financing Threshold: the minimum amount of the priced round that triggers conversion. Benchmark: $250K-$1M for pre-Seed, $1M+ for Seed. Warning threshold: a threshold that is too low (below $250K) can trigger conversion in a small bridge round that is not the intended Series A. The fifth metric is the Note-to-Cap Ratio: the total note principal divided by the Valuation Cap. Benchmark: below 30% is clean (e.g., $300K in notes on a $10M cap). Warning threshold: above 50% indicates the note holders will own a large percentage of the company at conversion.

CAPITAL STRUCTURE IMPLICATIONS

Convertible Notes create debt on the company's balance sheet, which has implications for future fundraising and for the company's financial appearance. The notes appear as a liability, not equity, and the interest accrual increases the liability over time. For a company with $2M in notes at 6% interest held for 18 months, the accrued interest is $180K, and the conversion amount is $2.18M. The maturity date creates a contingent liability: if the company cannot raise a qualified financing, it must negotiate with note holders from a position of weakness. Note holders can demand repayment, conversion at a lower valuation, or additional warrants and terms in exchange for an extension. The governance implication: prior to conversion, note holders are creditors, not shareholders. They have no voting rights, no board seats, and no protective provisions. However, the maturity date gives them a governance lever that SAFE holders lack: the ability to force a conversation about the company's trajectory at a known future date. The future fundraising implication: sophisticated Series A investors review the outstanding notes carefully. Notes that are approaching maturity without a clear path to a qualified financing are a red flag. Notes with onerous terms (high interest, aggressive caps, short maturity) suggest a company that was raising from a position of weakness. The tax implication: unlike a SAFE, a Convertible Note's interest accrual may have tax consequences. The company may be required to report accrued interest as an expense, and note holders may have taxable income from the accrued interest even before conversion.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

The use of Convertible Notes in the 2008-2010 period, before the creation of the SAFE, produced a series of "maturity cliff" situations during the post-financial-crisis fundraising environment. Companies that raised Convertible Notes in 2007-2008 with 12-18 month maturities faced the maturity date in 2009-2010, when the venture market was severely contracted. Many companies could not raise a qualified financing and faced note holders who had the legal right to demand repayment. The resulting negotiations often produced "recapitalizations" where note holders converted at significantly reduced caps, accepted additional warrants, or in extreme cases, took control of the company through a debt-to-equity conversion that wiped out common shareholders. The outcome: the industry learned that the maturity date, while a theoretical protection for investors, created a cliff that could destroy companies and value for all parties. This experience directly motivated Y Combinator's creation of the SAFE — an instrument without a maturity date, without interest, and without the debt structure that created the adversarial dynamic. The lesson: Convertible Notes are a more powerful instrument than SAFEs in terms of investor protections, but that power includes the power to destroy the company if the financing environment changes.

COMMON MISTAKES

The first mistake is "Maturity Date Ignorance": founders who raise Convertible Notes without tracking the maturity date, only to discover 30 days before maturity that they have no qualified financing and no plan for the note holders. The second mistake is "Interest Accrual Neglect": founders who model the note dilution using the principal only, forgetting that accrued interest increases the conversion amount and dilution. The third mistake is "Multiple Note Confusion": raising notes from multiple investors at different times with different caps, discounts, and maturity dates, creating a conversion waterfall that is even more complex than multiple SAFEs. The fourth mistake is "Note Stacking Without a Financing Plan": raising multiple notes without a clear timeline for the qualified financing, creating a situation where notes begin maturing sequentially and each maturity creates a crisis. The fifth mistake is "Qualified Financing Threshold Mismatch": setting the qualified financing threshold too low, triggering conversion on a small bridge round that was not intended to be the conversion event.

COUNTERINTUITIVE INSIGHT

The Convertible Note's maturity date, which is its primary structural advantage over the SAFE from an investor perspective, is also the feature most likely to destroy value for both investors and founders. A note approaching maturity creates a binary outcome: conversion at any price (which can be devastating to founders) or default (which can be devastating to both parties). The SAFE was designed to remove this binary outcome, and its adoption reflects the industry's learning that aligned instruments produce better outcomes than adversarial ones.

INVESTOR PERSPECTIVE

Positive Convertible Note signals: a note with a 24-month maturity, a cap that is reasonable relative to traction, an interest rate in the standard 2-8% range, and a founder who tracks the maturity date and has a clear financing plan that projects a qualified financing 6-12 months before maturity. Red flags: a note with less than 12 months to maturity and no qualified financing in process, multiple notes with different terms and maturity dates, a founder who cannot state the maturity date or accrued interest amount, and notes that represent more than 30% of the company at the cap conversion. Due diligence questions for note holders: "When is the maturity date, and what is your plan if a qualified financing has not occurred by that date?" "Show me the accrued interest on all outstanding notes and the total conversion amount." "Why did you choose Convertible Notes over SAFEs?" "Have you discussed an extension with your note holders?" Due diligence questions for Series A investors: "How many different note series are outstanding, and what are the terms of each?" "Have any notes passed their maturity date, and if so, what was the resolution?"

VENTUREVERSE EVALUATION SIGNALS

VentureVerse agents must evaluate Convertible Note structures for maturity risk and conversion complexity. Positive Signals: Notes with 24-month maturities and clear financing plans; a single note or a small number of notes on consistent terms; a founder who tracks the maturity date and accrued interest; a qualified financing projected 6-12 months before the earliest maturity; cumulative note dilution below 20%; interest rates in the standard range. Warning Signals: Notes with less than 12 months to maturity and no qualified financing in process; multiple notes with different maturity dates; a founder who cannot state the maturity date; cumulative note dilution above 30%; a qualified financing threshold that is too low; notes held by investors who are not professional venture investors and may not be sophisticated about conversion mechanics. Scoring Signals include a Convertible Note Health Score based on: maturity risk (40%), cumulative dilution (30%), and instrument consistency (30%). Risk Indicators: Any note that has passed its maturity date without resolution; notes representing more than 50% of the company at the cap; a founder who describes the notes as "like SAFEs" without understanding the differences; notes with interest rates above 10% or maturity dates below 12 months.

ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS

1. Create a Note Summary Table with columns: Investor, Principal, Interest Rate, Issuance Date, Maturity Date, Accrued Interest, Total Conversion Amount, Cap, Discount. Update accrued interest quarterly.
2. Set a calendar reminder for 6 months before each note's maturity date. If a qualified financing is not imminent at that point, begin conversations with note holders about extension or alternative conversion scenarios.
3. Model note conversion at a range of Series A valuations: calculate the conversion price under both the Cap and Discount methods, the number of shares, and the cumulative note dilution.
4. If you have multiple notes with different maturity dates, develop a plan to consolidate them — either through an amendment that aligns maturity dates or through a qualified financing that converts all notes simultaneously.
5. Include a qualified financing threshold that aligns with your intended Series A size, not a lower threshold that could be triggered by a small bridge round.
6. Negotiate for a 24-month maturity date at minimum; 18 months is the absolute floor. If an investor insists on 12 months, the instrument is inappropriate for a company that has not yet begun Series A conversations.
7. Consult with a tax advisor on the interest accrual. Understand whether the accrued interest creates taxable income for the company or the note holders, and plan accordingly.
8. If a note is approaching maturity and a qualified financing is not achievable, proactively propose an extension with a warrant or cap adjustment to compensate note holders, rather than waiting for the maturity date to force a negotiation from a position of weakness.
