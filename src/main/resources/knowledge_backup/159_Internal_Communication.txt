CORE CONCEPT

Internal Communication is the system of practices, channels, and norms through which information flows within an organization. The core concept is that communication is the nervous system of an organization. The speed, accuracy, and reach of internal communication determine the organization's ability to align, coordinate, and execute. The failure mode is "Communication by Osmosis": relying on informal, ad hoc communication (hallway conversations, water cooler chat, overheard discussions) that works at small scale but breaks down as the organization grows. For investors, a startup's internal communication systems are a signal of organizational scalability. A company with deliberate, scalable communication practices can coordinate a large, distributed organization; a company that relies on informal communication will fragment as it grows.

FRAMEWORK & MECHANICS

The Internal Communication framework, influenced by the practices of Stripe, Amazon, and GitLab, operates through three principles: Written-First (written communication is the default; verbal communication is supplementary), Broadcast and Narrowcast (information is broadcast widely by default and narrowed only when necessary), and Transparency (information is accessible to everyone by default; restricted only when legally or competitively necessary). The framework defines the communication channels: All-Hands (company-wide meetings for vision, strategy, and culture), Written Updates (weekly or monthly written updates from teams and executives), Documentation (a shared, searchable repository of decisions, processes, and knowledge), and Asynchronous Channels (Slack, email, discussion forums for ongoing communication).

The operational mechanism is the "Communication Audit": the company evaluates its internal communication channels for effectiveness, identifies gaps and overload points, and adjusts. The mental model is "communication is a product that you ship." Every email, update, and all-hands is a product that must deliver value to its audience. The decision process: the leadership team designs the communication system, models the communication practices, and continuously improves based on feedback.

ORGANIZATIONAL EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

Healthy internal communication: written communication is the default; information is broadcast broadly; the company has a shared documentation repository; communication channels are defined and managed; employees feel informed. Unhealthy: verbal communication dominates; information is siloed; no shared documentation; communication channels are chaotic; employees feel uninformed. Execution quality indicators: the speed of information flow, the accuracy of information as it propagates, employee feeling of being informed. Leadership quality indicators: the quality of leadership communication (all-hands, written updates). Organizational maturity indicators: the evolution from informal to structured communication.

KEY METRICS & BENCHMARKS

Benchmark 1: Employee Informedness. Above 80% of employees agree they have the information they need to do their job. Warning: below 50%. Benchmark 2: Documentation Coverage. Above 80% of key processes and decisions are documented. Warning: below 50%. Benchmark 3: All-Hands Attendance. Above 80% of employees attend or watch the recording of company all-hands. Warning: below 50%. Benchmark 4: Communication Overload. Below 20% of employees report feeling overwhelmed by communication volume. Warning: above 40%. Benchmark 5: Cross-Team Information Flow. Above 70% of employees agree they understand what other teams are working on. Warning: below 40%. Benchmark 6: Leadership Communication Rating. Above 70% of employees rate leadership communication as effective. Warning: below 40%.

OPERATING MODEL IMPLICATIONS

Internal communication directly determines how the organization aligns and coordinates. A company with written-first, transparent communication can align a large, distributed organization. A company with verbal, siloed communication will fragment. The leadership implication: leaders must be excellent communicators. The CEO's all-hands and written updates are the most important communication in the company. The team implication: teams that communicate well are aligned; teams that do not are fragmented. The governance implication: the board should receive regular, written communication from the company, not just verbal updates at board meetings. The scalability implication: informal communication breaks at approximately 30-50 employees. The company must transition to written-first, structured communication before this point. The execution implication: execution speed is a function of communication speed. Information that moves slowly creates slow decisions and slow execution.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

Stripe's internal communication culture, influenced by its founders' belief in writing as a tool for clarity, is a benchmark. Stripe uses written documents for strategy, decision-making, and updates. The company's "Writing Culture" values clear, concise, well-structured writing as a fundamental skill. All-hands are recorded and documented. Decisions are documented and searchable. The outcome: Stripe's communication practices have enabled it to scale to over 7,000 employees while maintaining alignment and velocity. The investor lesson: Stripe's investment in writing as a core competency is not a cultural preference; it is an operational system that enables coordination at scale.

COMMON MISTAKES

The first mistake is "Verbal-Only Culture": relying entirely on verbal communication that is lost, misinterpreted, and not searchable. The second mistake is "Information Silos": teams that communicate internally but not externally with other teams. The third mistake is "No Documentation": decisions and processes that live only in people's heads. The fourth mistake is "Over-Communication": flooding employees with so much information that they tune out. The fifth mistake is "Leader Silence": leaders who do not communicate regularly, leaving the organization to fill the void with rumors.

COUNTERINTUITIVE INSIGHT

The most important communication is the communication that feels repetitive. Leaders often stop communicating a message because they have said it before, but the organization needs to hear it many times before it is internalized. Repetition is not redundancy; it is reinforcement.

INVESTOR PERSPECTIVE

Positive communication signals: written-first culture; documented processes; transparent information flow; regular leadership communication; employees feel informed. Red flags: verbal-only; siloed information; no documentation; leader silence; employees uninformed. Due diligence questions: "How does information flow in your organization?" "What is your approach to written vs. verbal communication?" "How do you ensure cross-team information flow?" "How do you communicate strategy and decisions to the organization?" "How do you know if your communication is effective?"

ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS

1. Default to written communication. Every significant decision, strategy, and update should be documented in writing.
2. Build a shared, searchable documentation repository. Decisions and processes should be accessible to everyone.
3. Hold regular all-hands meetings where leadership communicates strategy, progress, and decisions transparently.
4. Broadcast information broadly. Default to sharing information with everyone unless there is a specific reason to restrict it.
5. Audit your communication channels. Identify which are effective and which are creating overload. Eliminate the overloaded ones.
6. Train your leaders in effective communication. Writing clearly and presenting effectively are skills that can be developed.
7. Repeat important messages. The organization needs to hear key messages multiple times before they are internalized.
8. For investors: Request the communication strategy, documentation coverage, and employee informedness data. A founder who cannot articulate how information flows has not designed their communication system.
