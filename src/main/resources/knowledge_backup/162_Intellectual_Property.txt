CORE CONCEPT

Intellectual Property (IP) is the set of legal rights that protect a company's intangible assets -- its inventions, creative works, brand identity, and trade secrets. The core concept is that IP is often a startup's most valuable asset, and its protection is essential to building a defensible business. The failure mode is "IP Neglect": failing to secure IP rights early, resulting in the loss of patent rights, trademark disputes, or the inability to prevent competitors from copying the company's core technology. For investors, a startup's IP position is a critical component of defensibility. A company with strong, well-protected IP has a moat; a company with weak or unprotected IP is vulnerable to competition.

FRAMEWORK & MECHANICS

The IP framework operates through four types of IP protection: Patents (protect inventions and technological innovations -- require filing with the patent office, are expensive and time-consuming, but provide strong protection for 20 years), Trademarks (protect brand names, logos, and slogans -- relatively inexpensive and provide indefinite protection if maintained), Copyrights (protect original creative works -- software code, designs, content -- automatically created upon creation but registration provides additional benefits), and Trade Secrets (protect confidential business information -- algorithms, customer lists, formulas -- protected by maintaining secrecy through NDAs and security measures).

The operational mechanism is the "IP Audit": the company identifies its IP assets, determines the appropriate protection for each, and implements the protection. The mental model is "IP is a strategic asset, not a legal expense." The decision to file a patent, register a trademark, or maintain a trade secret is a business decision, not a legal one. The decision process: the company evaluates its IP portfolio, prioritizes protection based on business value, and invests accordingly.

ORGANIZATIONAL EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

Healthy IP management: the company has identified its key IP assets; appropriate protection is in place for each; IP assignments are signed by all contributors; the company monitors for infringement; the IP strategy is aligned with the business strategy. Unhealthy: IP assets not identified; no protection in place; missing IP assignments; no infringement monitoring; IP treated as an afterthought. Execution quality indicators: the comprehensiveness of IP protection, the absence of IP disputes. Leadership quality indicators: the CTO or CEO's understanding of the company's IP position. Organizational maturity indicators: the company's IP portfolio and its alignment with strategy.

KEY METRICS & BENCHMARKS

Benchmark 1: Patent Filing Status. For deep tech startups, a provisional patent should be filed before any public disclosure. Warning: public disclosure before patent filing (destroys international patent rights). Benchmark 2: Trademark Registration. The company's name and logo should be trademarked in its primary markets. Warning: no trademark registration. Benchmark 3: IP Assignment Coverage. 100% of contributors must have signed IP assignments. Warning: any unsigned. Benchmark 4: Trade Secret Protection. Documented trade secret policies and NDAs for employees and partners. Warning: no trade secret policies. Benchmark 5: Open Source Compliance. If the company uses open source software, it must have a policy for open source license compliance. Warning: no open source policy. Benchmark 6: IP Dispute History. Zero active IP disputes is ideal. A history of resolved disputes is acceptable. Warning: active IP litigation.

OPERATING MODEL IMPLICATIONS

IP directly affects the company's defensibility and value. A company with strong IP can prevent competitors from copying its technology, charge premium prices, and command a higher valuation in an acquisition. The leadership implication: the CTO and CEO must own the IP strategy. It cannot be delegated entirely to legal counsel. The team implication: every employee and contractor must understand their obligations to protect company IP. The governance implication: the board should review the company's IP portfolio and strategy annually. The scalability implication: the IP strategy must evolve as the company's products and markets evolve. The execution implication: IP protection must be integrated into the product development process, not treated as a separate activity.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

Qualcomm's IP strategy, built on its foundational CDMA patents, is a case study in the value of IP. Qualcomm invested in patenting its wireless technology innovations early, building a patent portfolio that became the basis for its technology licensing business. The outcome: Qualcomm's IP portfolio has generated tens of billions of dollars in licensing revenue and has been a key component of the company's market cap above one hundred billion dollars. The investor lesson: Qualcomm's IP was not an afterthought; it was a core business strategy.

COMMON MISTAKES

The first mistake is "Public Disclosure Before Filing": disclosing an invention publicly before filing a patent application, destroying international patent rights. The second mistake is "No IP Assignments": failing to get IP assignments from founders, employees, and contractors. The third mistake is "No Trademark Registration": operating under a brand name that is not trademarked, creating the risk of a trademark dispute. The fourth mistake is "Ignoring Open Source Licenses": using open source software without understanding the license obligations. The fifth mistake is "No Trade Secret Protection": treating valuable confidential information without basic security measures.

COUNTERINTUITIVE INSIGHT

The most valuable IP is often not the patents but the trade secrets. A patent requires public disclosure of the invention; a trade secret can be protected indefinitely if secrecy is maintained. The Coca-Cola formula has been a trade secret for over 130 years, generating more value than any patent could have.

INVESTOR PERSPECTIVE

Positive IP signals: patents filed before public disclosure; trademarks registered; IP assignments complete; trade secret policies; open source compliance. Red flags: public disclosure before patent filing; no trademarks; missing IP assignments; no trade secret policies; open source violations. Due diligence questions: "What is your IP strategy, and how does it align with your business strategy?" "Do you have any patents filed or granted?" "Are your trademarks registered?" "Do you have signed IP assignments from all contributors?" "How do you protect your trade secrets?"

ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS

1. File a provisional patent application before any public disclosure of a potentially patentable invention. This preserves your patent rights.
2. Register your company name and logo as trademarks in your primary markets.
3. Ensure every founder, employee, and contractor who contributes IP has signed an IP assignment agreement.
4. Implement trade secret protection: NDAs, access controls, data security measures.
5. Develop an open source policy: know what open source licenses you are using and your obligations under them.
6. Conduct an annual IP audit: identify new IP assets and ensure they are protected.
7. Align your IP strategy with your business strategy. IP is a business asset, not a legal compliance exercise.
8. For investors: Request the IP portfolio, IP assignments, and IP strategy. A founder who cannot articulate their IP position has not protected their most valuable assets.
