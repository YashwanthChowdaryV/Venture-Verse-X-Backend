CORE CONCEPT

AI Agents are autonomous software systems capable of planning, reasoning, executing actions, and interacting with tools to achieve objectives. Unlike traditional AI applications that generate responses, agents perform tasks. The primary evaluation signal is not autonomy itself but whether autonomous execution creates measurable business value. Investors increasingly differentiate between true agentic systems and simple workflow automations. Successful AI agent startups reduce human labor, increase productivity, and integrate deeply into operational workflows. Failed startups frequently overestimate agent reliability or underestimate supervision requirements. The most important question is whether agents create durable operational advantages that improve as models and tooling evolve.

AI STARTUP FRAMEWORK & MECHANICS

The Agentic Systems Framework contains five components.

Reasoning Layer:
Planning and decision making.

Memory Layer:
Short-term and long-term context.

Tool Layer:
External actions and integrations.

Execution Layer:
Task completion.

Feedback Layer:
Learning and improvement.

A useful mental model is Human Labor Replacement Value.

Agent Value = Work Automated x Reliability x Frequency.

The strongest agents automate recurring workflows with clear economic value.

Strategically, AI agents become more valuable when integrated into systems of record and operational processes.

Agent businesses should focus on measurable outcomes rather than autonomous behavior alone.

AI EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

Agent startups should be evaluated across seven dimensions.

Task Reliability:
Accuracy and consistency.

Workflow Integration:
Operational importance.

Economic Impact:
Cost savings or revenue generation.

Scalability:
Ability to support large workloads.

Model Dependency:
Reliance on external providers.

Data Advantage:
Improvement through usage.

Autonomy Value:
Reduction in human intervention.

Strong agents generate repeatable business outcomes. Weak agents create impressive demonstrations but limited operational value.

ECONOMIC & MOAT ANALYSIS

Data moats improve agent performance.

Workflow moats emerge when agents become embedded in daily operations.

Distribution moats reduce acquisition costs.

Platform moats develop when agents orchestrate multiple systems.

Model commoditization often benefits agent businesses because better models increase reliability.

Margin durability depends on reducing supervision costs.

The strongest agent companies own operational workflows rather than model capabilities.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

Harvey demonstrates the agentic approach within legal workflows.

Rather than building a general-purpose assistant, Harvey focuses on legal research, drafting, and analysis.

Its differentiation comes from legal workflow integration and domain specialization.

As foundation models improve, Harvey benefits without rebuilding its product.

The investor lesson is that vertical agents often outperform generic assistants because they own valuable workflows.

COMMON MISTAKES

Many founders prioritize autonomy over reliability.

Others ignore workflow integration.

Some underestimate supervision requirements.

Many fail to measure economic impact.

Investors identify these weaknesses through retention and adoption metrics.

COUNTERINTUITIVE INSIGHT

The best AI agents are often less autonomous than founders expect. Reliability creates more value than maximum autonomy.

INVESTOR PERSPECTIVE

Positive signals include workflow ownership, measurable productivity gains, strong retention, and improving reliability.

Red flags include low task completion rates, weak adoption, and excessive supervision requirements.

Key diligence questions include:

What task is automated?

How much labor is removed?

How reliable is execution?

Required evidence includes workflow metrics, cost savings data, and customer retention.
