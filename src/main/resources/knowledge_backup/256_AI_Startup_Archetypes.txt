CORE CONCEPT

AI Startup Archetypes are the distinct categories of companies building products and services powered by artificial intelligence, each with fundamentally different business models, capital requirements, moats, and risk profiles. The core concept is that "AI startup" is not a single category. A foundation model company competing on model capability has a fundamentally different business than an AI application company competing on workflow integration. Classifying AI startups correctly is the prerequisite for evaluating them correctly. The failure mode is "AI as Monolith": applying the same evaluation framework to all AI startups, missing the structural differences that determine outcomes. VentureVerse agents use AI archetype classification to apply the appropriate benchmarks, identify the relevant risks, and generate stage-appropriate recommendations.

ARCHETYPE FRAMEWORK

The AI Startup Archetype framework classifies companies into five primary archetypes. Foundation Model Companies build large-scale AI models (LLMs, diffusion models, multimodal models) and monetize through API access, licensing, or enterprise agreements. Examples: OpenAI, Anthropic, Cohere, Mistral. Key characteristics: extreme capital intensity (hundreds of millions to billions for training), commodity risk (models are improving rapidly and open source alternatives are emerging), winner-take-most dynamics, moats from data flywheels and distribution. AI Infrastructure Companies build the tools and platforms for developing, deploying, and managing AI. Examples: vector databases (Pinecone, Weaviate), orchestration frameworks (LangChain), monitoring (Weights & Biases), inference optimization. Key characteristics: picks-and-shovels economics, SaaS-like margins, benefit from AI adoption regardless of which models win. AI Application Companies build AI-powered products for specific use cases. Examples: AI copilots (GitHub Copilot), AI agents (Devin), AI-powered SaaS (Jasper, Copy.ai). Key characteristics: moderate capital intensity, distribution is critical, model independence is a key defensibility question, workflow integration creates switching costs. AI Vertical Solutions build AI for specific industries. Examples: legal AI (Harvey), medical AI (pathology, radiology), financial AI (fraud detection, underwriting). Key characteristics: domain expertise moat, proprietary data advantages, regulatory barriers create defensibility. AI Agent Companies build autonomous systems that perform complex tasks. Examples: customer service agents, coding agents, sales agents. Key characteristics: emerging category, reliability is the primary challenge, workflow integration determines adoption.

ARCHETYPE CLASSIFICATION MODEL

The classification model operates through a decision tree. First, determine the primary revenue model: API/Usage (Foundation Model or AI Infrastructure), SaaS Subscription (AI Application or AI Vertical), Transaction/Outcome (AI Agent). Second, determine the primary customer: Developers (AI Infrastructure), Enterprises horizontal (AI Application), Enterprises vertical (AI Vertical), Consumers (AI Application). Third, determine the capital intensity: Extreme (Foundation Model), Moderate (AI Infrastructure, AI Vertical), Variable (AI Application, AI Agent). Fourth, determine the primary moat: Model Quality (Foundation Model), Ecosystem/Integration (AI Infrastructure), Workflow/Data (AI Application), Domain Expertise/Data (AI Vertical), Reliability/Integration (AI Agent). Edge cases: companies that span archetypes (OpenAI is both Foundation Model and AI Application with ChatGPT), companies transitioning between archetypes, and companies creating new archetypes. Hybrid classification is applied when a company has characteristics of multiple archetypes, with a primary archetype assigned based on the dominant revenue source and moat.

SUCCESS PATTERNS

Foundation Model success pattern: achieve model leadership early, convert into platform with developer ecosystem, expand to applications to capture more value, build data flywheel from usage. AI Infrastructure success pattern: become the default tool in the AI development stack, integrate with all major model providers, expand from point solution to platform, achieve SaaS-like NRR above 120%. AI Application success pattern: build deep workflow integration, accumulate proprietary data from usage, maintain model independence, expand from single use case to platform. AI Vertical success pattern: leverage domain expertise to build product that generalists cannot replicate, accumulate proprietary industry data, navigate regulatory requirements, expand to adjacent verticals. AI Agent success pattern: achieve reliability above human-level for specific tasks, integrate deeply into enterprise workflows, build trust through transparency and control. Common across archetypes: the most successful AI companies are not those with the best models but those with the best products, distribution, and data accumulation.

FAILURE PATTERNS

Foundation Model failure pattern: commoditization by open source, inability to monetize API access at sustainable margins, capital requirements exceeding available funding, platform companies (Google, Microsoft, Meta) offering models for free. AI Infrastructure failure pattern: being absorbed into broader platforms (Databricks, Snowflake adding vector search), cloud providers offering native services, point solutions without expansion path. AI Application failure pattern: thin wrappers on third-party models without defensibility, model dependency creating existential risk when the model provider becomes a competitor, novelty-driven adoption with low retention, inference costs making unit economics unviable. AI Vertical failure pattern: insufficient domain expertise, regulatory barriers underestimated, long sales cycles exhausting runway, horizontal AI applications entering the vertical. AI Agent failure pattern: reliability below acceptable threshold for enterprise adoption, failure to integrate with existing enterprise systems, trust incidents destroying adoption.

KEY METRICS & BENCHMARKS

Benchmark 1: Gross Margin. Foundation Model: 30-60% (compute-intensive). AI Infrastructure: 60-85%. AI Application: 60-85%. AI Vertical: 60-85%. AI Agent: 40-70%. Warning: below 50% for Application/Vertical. Benchmark 2: Net Revenue Retention. Foundation Model: above 120% for API. AI Infrastructure: above 120%. AI Application: above 110%. AI Vertical: above 115%. AI Agent: above 110%. Warning: below 100% for any archetype. Benchmark 3: Model Dependency Score. Low (model-swappable or proprietary) is strong for Application and Vertical. High (single model provider) is risky. Benchmark 4: Capital Required to First Revenue. Foundation Model: above one hundred million dollars. AI Infrastructure: ten to fifty million dollars. AI Application: one to ten million dollars. AI Vertical: five to twenty million dollars. AI Agent: five to twenty million dollars. Warning: exceeding archetype benchmark without a clear path. Benchmark 5: Proprietary Data Accumulation. Measured as the percentage of model improvement attributable to customer-specific data. Above 20% is a data moat. Benchmark 6: Inference Cost as Percentage of Revenue. Below 10% is strong; 10-30% is acceptable. Warning: above 30%. Benchmark 7: Revenue Growth Rate. Foundation Model: above 100% at scale. AI Infrastructure: above 80%. AI Application: above 80%. AI Vertical: above 60%. AI Agent: above 80%.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES

OpenAI (Foundation Model + AI Application hybrid): classified primarily as Foundation Model based on dominant revenue from API access and model licensing. Secondary classification as AI Application (ChatGPT). The hybrid classification is appropriate because OpenAI spans both archetypes. Anthropic (Foundation Model): pure Foundation Model focused on safety and enterprise. Pinecone (AI Infrastructure): vector database for AI applications. GitHub Copilot (AI Application): AI copilot integrated into developer workflow. Harvey (AI Vertical): AI for legal services, leveraging domain expertise and proprietary legal data. Each example illustrates the archetype classification logic and the different evaluation frameworks required.

COMMON MISCLASSIFICATIONS

The first misclassification is "AI Wrapper as AI Application": classifying a thin UI on a third-party model as an AI Application when it lacks the workflow integration, data accumulation, or switching costs of a true application. The second is "Feature as AI Agent": classifying a simple automation as an AI Agent when it lacks autonomy and complex task completion. The third is "Vertical as Horizontal": classifying an AI Vertical solution as an AI Application when its domain specificity is its primary characteristic. The fourth is "Infrastructure as Foundation Model": classifying an AI Infrastructure company as a Foundation Model company when it does not train its own models. The fifth is "Hybrid as Pure": failing to recognize when a company spans multiple archetypes.

COUNTERINTUITIVE INSIGHT

The most valuable AI companies may not be Foundation Model providers. Application-layer and Vertical AI companies often capture customer value more efficiently, have better unit economics, and face less commoditization risk. The Foundation Model layer is capturing mindshare and capital, but the Application layer may capture the majority of the long-term value.

INVESTOR PERSPECTIVE

Most attractive archetypes for venture: AI Infrastructure (picks and shovels, SaaS-like economics, model-independent), AI Vertical (domain moat, proprietary data, regulatory barriers). Moderate attractiveness: AI Application (large opportunity but commoditization risk), AI Agent (emerging, unproven reliability). Highest risk: Foundation Model (extreme capital intensity, commoditization risk, big tech competition). Due diligence implications: Foundation Model diligence focuses on model quality trajectory and capital requirements. AI Application diligence focuses on distribution, switching costs, and model independence. AI Vertical diligence focuses on domain expertise and regulatory strategy. Return profiles vary dramatically by archetype: Foundation Models require the largest outcomes to justify the capital; AI Applications can generate attractive returns with more moderate outcomes.

ARCHETYPE DIAGNOSTICS

Positive Signals: Clear archetype classification, alignment between business model and archetype, metrics above archetype benchmarks, defensibility appropriate for archetype. Warning Signals: Ambiguous classification, business model mismatch with archetype, metrics below benchmarks, defensibility gaps. Scaling Indicators: for Foundation Models, model improvement trajectory; for Applications, distribution efficiency; for Infrastructure, ecosystem adoption; for Vertical, domain penetration. Capital Efficiency Indicators: for Applications, CAC payback under 12 months; for Infrastructure, burn multiple below 1.5x. Moat Indicators: for Applications, workflow integration and data accumulation; for Infrastructure, ecosystem and switching costs; for Vertical, domain data and regulatory barriers. Risk Indicators: model dependency, commoditization, big tech competition, inference costs.

VENTUREVERSE CLASSIFICATION SIGNALS

Classification Signals: revenue model type, primary customer type, capital intensity, primary moat type, model dependency level. Confidence Signals: clarity of archetype characteristics, consistency across classification dimensions, stability of classification over time. Risk Signals: archetype mismatch, archetype drift, hybrid complexity, commoditization exposure. Growth Signals: revenue growth relative to archetype benchmark, NRR relative to archetype benchmark. Fundability Signals: capital requirements relative to archetype norm, revenue scale relative to stage. Recommendation Signals: archetype-appropriate investment structure, archetype-specific risk mitigation. Archetype Fit Signals: alignment between the startup's execution and the archetype's success patterns.

ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS

1. Classify every AI startup into its primary archetype before applying evaluation frameworks. The archetype determines the relevant benchmarks, risks, and investment approach.
2. For Application-layer startups, assess model dependency as a primary risk factor. A startup dependent on a single model provider with no switching capability is carrying existential risk.
3. For Foundation Model startups, model the capital requirements to achieve and maintain model leadership. If the required capital exceeds available venture funding, the model is not venture-fundable.
4. For Vertical AI startups, evaluate domain expertise as rigorously as technology. A great AI model applied without domain expertise will fail against a good model applied with deep domain knowledge.
5. Detect archetype drift early. A startup that begins as an AI Application and starts training its own models is changing archetypes and requires a different evaluation.
6. For AI Agent startups, assess reliability quantitatively. The difference between 90% and 99% reliability is the difference between a demo and a product.
7. Monitor inference costs as a percentage of revenue. If they are above 30% and not declining, the unit economics of the AI archetype are broken.
8. For hybrid startups, evaluate each archetype component separately and weight the evaluation by revenue contribution.
