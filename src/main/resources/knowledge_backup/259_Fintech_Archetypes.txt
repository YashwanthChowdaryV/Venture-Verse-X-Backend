CORE CONCEPT

Fintech Archetypes are the distinct categories of financial technology companies, each with fundamentally different business models, regulatory exposures, capital requirements, and risk profiles. The core concept is that "fintech" spans an enormous range -- from payments infrastructure to consumer lending to insurtech to embedded finance -- and each sub-category has unique success factors and failure modes. Classifying fintech startups correctly is essential because the evaluation framework for a payments company (infrastructure-like, network effects, low credit risk) is fundamentally different from the evaluation framework for a lending company (balance sheet risk, credit cycles, regulatory capital requirements). The failure mode is "Fintech as Uniform": applying the same investment criteria to all fintech companies. VentureVerse agents use fintech archetype classification to apply the appropriate risk models, regulatory assessments, and return expectations.

ARCHETYPE FRAMEWORK

The Fintech Archetype framework classifies companies across three primary dimensions. Financial Activity Dimension: what financial service does the company provide? Payments (moving money), Lending (providing credit), Insurance (underwriting risk), Wealth and Asset Management (managing investments), Banking (deposit-taking and lending), Capital Markets (trading and issuance), or Infrastructure (enabling other fintechs). Risk Exposure Dimension: does the company take financial risk onto its balance sheet? Balance Sheet Risk (credit risk, market risk, insurance risk) vs. Agency/Platform (no financial risk, earns fees). Regulatory Dimension: what regulatory regime applies? Banking Regulation, Securities Regulation, Insurance Regulation, Money Transmission, or Limited Regulation. The combination of these dimensions defines the archetype.

ARCHETYPE CLASSIFICATION MODEL

The classification model operates through a sequential process. First, identify the primary financial activity. Second, determine the risk exposure: Balance Sheet or Agency/Platform. Third, identify the applicable regulatory regime. The primary archetypes are: Payments Infrastructure (Agency, Money Transmission) -- Stripe, Adyen, Plaid. Lending Platform (Agency or Light Balance Sheet, Lending Regulation) -- LendingClub, Affirm. Lending Balance Sheet (Balance Sheet, Banking Regulation) -- neobanks, credit card issuers. Insurtech MGA (Agency, Insurance Regulation) -- managing general agents that underwrite and distribute. Insurtech Carrier (Balance Sheet, Insurance Regulation) -- full-stack insurance companies. WealthTech (Agency, Securities Regulation) -- Robinhood, Betterment. Banking-as-a-Service (Agency, Banking Regulation) -- Synapse, Unit. Embedded Finance (Agency, Varies) -- companies enabling non-financial companies to offer financial products.

SUCCESS PATTERNS

Payments Infrastructure success pattern: become the rails for a large volume of transactions, achieve scale economies in processing, expand to adjacent services (lending, fraud, data), build network effects. Lending Platform success pattern: originate loans efficiently using technology, distribute to capital partners, avoid balance sheet risk, build data advantages in underwriting. Banking-as-a-Service success pattern: build the infrastructure layer for embedded finance, achieve scale across multiple clients, navigate regulatory complexity as a moat. Insurtech MGA success pattern: use technology for better underwriting and pricing, distribute through embedded partnerships, avoid balance sheet risk. WealthTech success pattern: aggregate assets through superior user experience, monetize through fees, interest, and payment for order flow, achieve scale to spread fixed costs.

FAILURE PATTERNS

Lending Balance Sheet failure pattern: grow loan book aggressively without adequate underwriting, experience credit losses during downturn, capital insufficient to absorb losses. Insurtech Carrier failure pattern: underprice risk to gain market share, experience catastrophic losses, reinsurance insufficient. Payments Commoditization failure pattern: undifferentiated payment processing with low take rates, unable to achieve scale. Regulatory Failure: business model depends on regulatory interpretation that is rejected, or regulatory change eliminates the business model. Embedded Finance Dependency: building on a single Banking-as-a-Service provider, vulnerable to provider changes. Unit Economics Failure: CAC exceeding customer lifetime value, especially in consumer fintech with high churn.

KEY METRICS & BENCHMARKS

Benchmark 1: Take Rate (Payments). Above 1% for payments processing; above 2% for value-added payments. Warning: below 0.5%. Benchmark 2: Net Interest Margin (Lending). Above 3% is healthy. Warning: below 1%. Benchmark 3: Loan Loss Rate (Lending). Below 3% annually for consumer; below 1% for SMB. Warning: above 5% consumer. Benchmark 4: Combined Ratio (Insurance). Below 100% indicates underwriting profit; below 95% is strong. Warning: above 105%. Benchmark 5: Gross Margin (Infrastructure). Above 60% for fintech infrastructure. Warning: below 40%. Benchmark 6: Customer Acquisition Cost Payback (Consumer Fintech). Under 12 months. Warning: above 24 months. Benchmark 7: Regulatory Compliance Cost as Percentage of Revenue. Below 5% efficient. Warning: above 10%.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES

Stripe (Payments Infrastructure, Agency, Money Transmission): the canonical payments infrastructure company. Affirm (Lending Platform, Light Balance Sheet, Lending Regulation): point-of-sale lending platform. Lemonade (Insurtech Carrier, Balance Sheet, Insurance Regulation): full-stack digital insurer. Robinhood (WealthTech, Agency, Securities Regulation): commission-free trading platform. Plaid (Infrastructure, Agency, Money Transmission): financial data connectivity. Each example illustrates different risk exposures and regulatory regimes.

COMMON MISCLASSIFICATIONS

The first misclassification is "Platform vs. Balance Sheet": classifying a lending platform that retains some risk as purely Agency. The second is "Regulatory Optimism": underestimating the regulatory requirements for a given financial activity. The third is "Fintech vs. SaaS": classifying a fintech infrastructure company as pure SaaS. The fourth is "Risk Aggregation": failing to recognize when a fintech is accumulating risk through multiple activities. The fifth is "Archetype Drift": a company evolving from Agency to Balance Sheet without updating its risk management.

COUNTERINTUITIVE INSIGHT

The most valuable fintech companies are often not those that take the most financial risk. The payments infrastructure companies (Stripe, Adyen) have captured more value than most balance-sheet-intensive fintechs. The agency model -- earning fees without taking financial risk -- has proven to be the superior business model in fintech.

INVESTOR PERSPECTIVE

Most attractive archetypes: Payments Infrastructure (network effects, capital-light), Fintech Infrastructure (picks and shovels, SaaS-like), Insurtech MGA (capital-light, technology moat). Moderate attractiveness: WealthTech (AUM-based, market correlation), Lending Platform (capital-light, credit cycle exposure). Highest risk: Lending Balance Sheet (credit risk, capital intensity), Insurtech Carrier (underwriting risk, regulatory capital). Due diligence implications: for Balance Sheet, assess risk management and capital adequacy; for Agency, assess platform risk and take rate sustainability; for all, assess regulatory strategy. Return profiles: Payments can generate the highest multiples; Lending generates higher revenue per customer but lower multiples.

ARCHETYPE DIAGNOSTICS

Positive Signals: Clear archetype classification, appropriate risk management for archetype, regulatory clarity, metrics above archetype benchmarks. Warning Signals: Risk exposure mismatch with archetype, regulatory ambiguity, metrics below benchmarks. Capital Efficiency Indicators: for Agency, revenue per employee; for Balance Sheet, return on equity. Moat Indicators: for Payments, network effects; for Lending, underwriting data; for Infrastructure, switching costs. Risk Indicators: credit losses, regulatory actions, fraud losses, platform dependency.

VENTUREVERSE CLASSIFICATION SIGNALS

Classification Signals: primary financial activity, risk exposure type, regulatory regime, balance sheet intensity. Confidence Signals: regulatory clarity, risk management maturity. Risk Signals: credit risk exposure, regulatory uncertainty, platform dependency. Growth Signals: transaction volume growth, revenue growth, customer growth. Fundability Signals: capital adequacy, regulatory standing, unit economics. Recommendation Signals: archetype-appropriate investment structure, risk-adjusted return expectations. Archetype Fit Signals: alignment between business model and archetype risk profile.

ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS

1. Classify every fintech startup by financial activity, risk exposure, and regulatory regime. The evaluation framework for each archetype is fundamentally different.
2. Distinguish between Agency and Balance Sheet risk exposure. The capital requirements, risk management, and return profiles are different.
3. Assess regulatory strategy as a primary evaluation criterion. A fintech with regulatory ambiguity is carrying existential risk.
4. For lending startups, stress-test the loan portfolio for a severe credit downturn. Most fintech lenders have not been tested by a recession.
5. For payments startups, evaluate take rate sustainability. Payment take rates are under continuous pressure from competitors and regulation.
6. For insurtech startups, evaluate underwriting capability separate from technology capability. Great technology with poor underwriting is a money-losing machine.
7. Monitor archetype drift. A fintech that evolves from Agency to Balance Sheet requires a fundamentally different evaluation.
8. Apply archetype-specific risk factors in the VentureVerse Risk Assessment Framework. Each fintech archetype has unique risks that must be weighted accordingly.
