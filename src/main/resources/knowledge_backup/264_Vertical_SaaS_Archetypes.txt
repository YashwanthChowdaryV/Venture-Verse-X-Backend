CORE CONCEPT

Vertical SaaS Archetypes are the distinct categories of software companies built for specific industries, each with fundamentally different TAM dynamics, regulatory environments, customer concentration, and expansion patterns. The core concept is that vertical SaaS is not a single category but a collection of industry-specific software models that differ in market size, regulatory complexity, and monetization potential. Classifying vertical SaaS startups correctly is essential for evaluating TAM adequacy, expansion potential, and the defensibility of domain expertise. The failure mode is "Vertical as Small Horizontal": evaluating vertical SaaS as simply a smaller version of horizontal SaaS, missing the unique dynamics of industry-specific software. VentureVerse agents use vertical SaaS archetype classification to apply appropriate TAM thresholds, expansion expectations, and moat assessments.

ARCHETYPE FRAMEWORK

The Vertical SaaS Archetype framework classifies companies across three primary dimensions. Industry TAM Dimension: Large Vertical (TAM above five billion dollars -- healthcare, financial services, construction, legal), Medium Vertical (TAM one to five billion dollars -- restaurants, real estate, insurance, education), or Small Vertical (TAM below one billion dollars -- niche industries, specialized professions). Regulatory Intensity Dimension: High Regulation (healthcare, financial services, legal, insurance -- compliance creates barriers), Medium Regulation (construction, real estate, education -- some regulatory requirements), or Low Regulation (restaurants, retail, services -- limited regulatory barriers). Expansion Potential Dimension: Multi-Product within Vertical (expanding from a single workflow to a platform within the industry), Adjacent Vertical (expanding from one vertical to related verticals), Embedded Fintech (adding payments, lending, or insurance within the vertical), or Single Product (limited expansion potential). The combination defines the archetype.

ARCHETYPE CLASSIFICATION MODEL

The classification model operates through a sequential process. First, determine the industry TAM. Second, assess the regulatory intensity. Third, evaluate the expansion potential. Primary archetypes include: Large + High Regulation + Multi-Product (Veeva, Epic, Athenahealth) -- healthcare and life sciences platforms. Large + High Regulation + Embedded Fintech (vertical fintech for regulated industries). Medium + Low Regulation + Multi-Product (Toast, Procore, ServiceTitan) -- restaurant, construction, and field service platforms. Medium + Low Regulation + Embedded Fintech (Toast, ShopKeep) -- vertical SaaS with payments. Small + Low Regulation + Single Product (niche vertical SaaS) -- limited venture-scale potential unless expansion is possible.

SUCCESS PATTERNS

Large + High Regulation + Multi-Product success pattern: build deep domain expertise, navigate regulatory complexity as a moat, expand from a single workflow to a platform, achieve high switching costs. Medium + Low Regulation + Multi-Product success pattern: modernize a legacy industry, build a comprehensive platform, add embedded fintech to multiply revenue per customer. Medium + Embedded Fintech success pattern: software as the system of record, payments and financial services as the revenue multiplier, achieve SaaS-like margins with fintech-like revenue. Small + Adjacent Vertical success pattern: dominate a small vertical, expand to adjacent verticals using the same platform, achieve venture-scale through aggregation.

FAILURE PATTERNS

TAM Ceiling: vertical TAM too small to support venture-scale outcomes, growth stalls after capturing the initial market. Regulatory Surprise: underestimating the regulatory complexity of the vertical, compliance costs eroding margins. Domain Expertise Gap: building software without deep industry knowledge, product fails to meet industry-specific requirements. Services Creep: requiring extensive consulting and implementation, gross margins decline. Incumbent Lock-In: unable to displace entrenched legacy vendors with high switching costs. Single Product Ceiling: capturing a single workflow without expanding to a platform, vulnerable to competitors with broader offerings.

KEY METRICS & BENCHMARKS

Benchmark 1: Vertical TAM. Above five billion dollars is strong; one to five billion is adequate; below one billion requires adjacent expansion. Warning: below five hundred million without expansion path. Benchmark 2: Gross Margin. Software: 70-85%. With services: 50-70%. Warning: below 50%. Benchmark 3: Net Revenue Retention. Above 110% strong; above 120% exceptional. Warning: below 100%. Benchmark 4: Revenue Per Customer. Above ten thousand dollars annually is strong for vertical SaaS. Warning: below five thousand dollars. Benchmark 5: Embedded Fintech Revenue Percentage. Above 20% of revenue from fintech is strong. Benchmark 6: Market Share in Vertical. Above 10% is meaningful; above 30% is dominant. Benchmark 7: Regulatory Compliance Cost. Below 5% of revenue is efficient. Warning: above 10%.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES

Veeva (Large + High Regulation + Multi-Product): life sciences cloud platform. Procore (Medium + Low Regulation + Multi-Product): construction management platform. Toast (Medium + Low Regulation + Embedded Fintech): restaurant platform with payments. ServiceTitan (Medium + Low Regulation + Multi-Product): field service management. Squire (Small + Low Regulation + Embedded Fintech): barbershop management with payments. Each example illustrates different TAM sizes, regulatory environments, and expansion strategies.

COMMON MISCLASSIFICATIONS

The first misclassification is "Vertical as Horizontal": classifying a vertical SaaS as a horizontal SaaS because it serves multiple functions within an industry. The second is "TAM Overestimation": overestimating the vertical TAM by including adjacent markets that are not addressable. The third is "Regulatory Underestimation": underestimating the regulatory complexity of an industry. The fourth is "Expansion Assumption": assuming a vertical SaaS can easily expand to adjacent verticals. The fifth is "Embedded Fintech Assumption": assuming fintech revenue will materialize without the required partnerships and regulatory approvals.

COUNTERINTUITIVE INSIGHT

The best vertical SaaS markets are often those that look too small to attract horizontal competitors. A two billion dollar vertical that Salesforce ignores is a protected market where a focused startup can build a dominant position. The small market becomes a moat.

INVESTOR PERSPECTIVE

Most attractive archetypes: Large + High Regulation (regulatory moat, large TAM, high switching costs), Medium + Embedded Fintech (software revenue plus fintech revenue, higher revenue per customer). Moderate: Medium + Multi-Product (adequate TAM, expansion potential). Highest risk: Small without Expansion (TAM ceiling), High Regulation without Domain Expertise (compliance risk). Due diligence implications: assess TAM bottom-up, evaluate regulatory strategy, assess domain expertise, evaluate expansion potential. Return profiles: Large + High Regulation can generate the largest outcomes; Medium + Embedded Fintech can generate the highest revenue per customer.

ARCHETYPE DIAGNOSTICS

Positive Signals: TAM above one billion dollars, regulatory clarity, deep domain expertise, multiple expansion vectors, metrics above benchmarks. Warning Signals: TAM below five hundred million dollars, regulatory uncertainty, domain expertise gap, limited expansion, metrics below benchmarks. Scaling Indicators: vertical market share, customer count growth, expansion product adoption. Capital Efficiency Indicators: CAC payback, gross margin, services percentage. Moat Indicators: domain expertise, regulatory barriers, switching costs, embedded fintech. Risk Indicators: TAM ceiling, regulatory changes, incumbent response, services creep.

VENTUREVERSE CLASSIFICATION SIGNALS

Classification Signals: industry TAM, regulatory intensity, expansion potential, domain expertise depth. Confidence Signals: TAM methodology, regulatory clarity, expansion evidence. Risk Signals: TAM ceiling, regulatory uncertainty, domain expertise gaps. Growth Signals: revenue growth, market share growth, expansion revenue. Fundability Signals: TAM adequacy, regulatory standing, unit economics. Recommendation Signals: archetype-appropriate TAM thresholds and expansion expectations. Archetype Fit Signals: alignment between vertical characteristics and business model.

ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS

1. Classify every vertical SaaS startup by TAM, regulatory intensity, and expansion potential. The evaluation framework for a Large + High Regulation vertical is fundamentally different from a Small + Low Regulation vertical.
2. Size the vertical TAM bottom-up. Count the customers, estimate the revenue per customer, and validate with primary research.
3. Assess regulatory complexity as both a barrier and a moat. High regulation creates barriers to entry but also creates compliance costs.
4. Evaluate domain expertise as rigorously as technology. A vertical SaaS without deep industry expertise is vulnerable to competitors who have it.
5. Identify expansion vectors: multi-product within vertical, adjacent verticals, or embedded fintech. A single-product vertical SaaS has a ceiling.
6. Model the embedded fintech opportunity. Payments, lending, and insurance can multiply revenue per customer.
7. Monitor services percentage. If services revenue is above 20% of total, the gross margin is eroding.
8. Apply archetype-specific TAM thresholds. A Small vertical requires a credible adjacent expansion path to be venture-fundable.
